# Parlor

**Room ID:** `parlor`
**Floor:** Ground (0)

---

## Narrative Purpose

Lady Ashworth's domain — a space for receiving guests, afternoon tea, and maintaining social appearances. Here, the facade cracks. The torn diary page is the **FIRST MAJOR CLUE** — the player learns someone is locked in the attic. This room triggers the phase transition from Exploration → Discovery.

The tea service is now a real tableau instead of a stand-in. A low tea table, emptied pot, one used cup, and one untouched cup sit between the settees so the room can actually show the social ritual its text keeps invoking.

The parlor establishes:
- **Lady Ashworth** — her portrait in mourning dress before any deaths
- **The hidden child** — diary page about "whispers from the attic" 
- **Elizabeth's echo** — the music box that plays on its own later
- **First warmth reclaimed by hand** — the hearth becomes the first stable fire the player makes in the house
- **Arsenic green** — walls that literally poison. Beauty concealing danger.

**Visual:** Arsenic green walls (wall_4, #335938). Wood parquet floor (floor1). Plaster with molding ceiling (cieling1). The fireplace begins dark; the player wakes it and pulls the first firebrand from it. Two velvet settees angle toward the hearth in a proper conversation arrangement, Lady Ashworth's writing desk sits offset to the left, and small tables hold the music box and torn diary page where the player can actually read the room as a domestic stage.

**Audio:** Before the hearth is reclaimed, the room is almost too still. After it is lit: low fire crackle, soft footsteps on parquet, and no clock ticking. After `elizabeth_aware`: faint music box melody at the edge of hearing.

**Tone:** Intimate compared to foyer. Lady Ashworth's prison of propriety. The first warmth here is an act of occupation, not a gift from the house.

### Color Symbolism
Arsenic green wallpaper was literally toxic in Victorian England — the dye released arsenic fumes. Lady Ashworth CHOSE this color. The family's nature: attractive on the surface, deadly beneath. Warm fireplace orange fighting against poison green.
