# Player Premise

This document records the current canonical playable-present framing for
Ashworth Manor. It exists to keep script, runtime, room docs, and task batches
aligned while the implementation catches up.

---

## Playable Present

- The game takes place shortly after the Victorian collapse of the Ashworth
  household, close enough that the estate still stands intact and the family's
  social position is still legible.
- The manor is not a modern ruin.
- The house should feel recently dropped rather than centuries abandoned:
  maintained until the caretaker's death, then left in strained stillness.
- The estate is a burden, not a dream inheritance or likely new domicile.

---

## Player Position

- The player is an Ashworth descendant and already knows that fact.
- They are an established adult with a life elsewhere, not a drifter with no
  standing.
- They likely arrive from London by rail and hired carriage.
- They visited the estate as a child, then were abruptly kept away.
- They do not return to discover their lineage. They return to discover what
  was hidden from it.

---

## Inciting Packet

- The opening document is not a single melodramatic note. It is an inner page
  from a larger solicitor's packet.
- The packet is formal, professional, external, and neutral. It is not "from
  the house."
- The estate has already passed to the player by the time the game begins.
- The packet's immediate narrative job is to confirm inheritance, duty, and
  personal attendance after the death of the final caretaker.
- The wider packet should later exist as an inventory document bundle and may
  include:
  - an estate schedule or survey-like overview
  - visible amendments or redactions
  - later-usable documentary clues

---

## Opening Flow

1. The player reads the relevant inheritance page from the solicitor's packet.
2. First-person resolves at the front gate as the hired cab departs.
3. The player's valise sits beneath the estate sign where the driver left it.
4. No one is present to receive the player.
5. The player opens the valise and establishes diegetic inventory.
6. The valise provides the front-door key, the packet, essential personal
   effects, and the brass winding key tied to Elizabeth's music box.
7. The player crosses the gate, walks the long approach, and unlocks the front
   door personally.

The front gate is the first embodied room, but not the first narrative beat.

---

## Gate and Grounds Rules

- The sign is neutral estate signage, not supernatural graffiti.
- The sign wording remains:
  - `Enter the Grounds`
  - `Resume the Visit`
  - `Adjust the House`
- The estate should present as wealthy, formal, and mostly respectable on first
  view.
- The long drive remains singular until the forecourt/front steps.
- At the forecourt the estate opens spatially, but the side routes are visibly
  gated and initially unavailable.
- The mansion is mostly withheld on the first approach. Emptiness, distance,
  and lamp glow do the work.

---

## House Lighting Doctrine

- The mansion is dark on arrival.
- Exterior practicals may still burn.
- Interior light is initially the player's responsibility.
- The house is a gas-and-oil estate, not a brightly electric one.
- Early light is intimate and fragile.
- Midgame light becomes a stable estate state once the player reclaims the
  service-side gas system.
- Late game must be able to take that stability away again.

---

## Memory Layers

Ashworth Manor now carries three overlapping layers of time:

1. Embodied present return
   - the player arrives as heir and must physically take possession
2. Childhood memory
   - the player once knew the estate from a child's scale and route logic
3. Elizabeth's life truth
   - the concealed shape of Elizabeth's existence and death across the three
     authored storylines

Not every flashback should be Victorian tableau. Some should be the player's
own childhood recollection.

---

## Emotional Frame

- The player belongs here legally, but not comfortably.
- The house should feel familiar before it feels understood.
- Early dread comes from etiquette failure, emptiness, distance, and
  incompleteness rather than overt supernatural display.
- Elizabeth's presence should first feel like a pressure on space and memory,
  not a parade of jumpscares.
